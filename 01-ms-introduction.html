1  Introduction to Mass Spectrometry

1.1 Mass spectrometry for data analysts

In this book, we view mass spectrometry as a quantitative sensor that converts chemical mixtures into digital data: spectra, chromatograms, and feature tables. Rather than focusing on detailed engineering, our goal is to provide enough physical intuition that you can understand what is in your raw files and how experimental choices constrain downstream analysis.

At its core, a mass spectrometer measures the mass‑to‑charge ratio ((m/z)) of gas‑phase ions. Neutral molecules are first converted into ions by an ion source, then separated according to (m/z) by a mass analyzer operating under high vacuum, and finally detected as electrical signals. When coupled to chromatographic separations such as LC or GC, this produces a time series of mass spectra from which we extract chromatographic peaks and build the feature matrices that underpin statistical analyses throughout this book.

Most of the datasets you will encounter—proteomics, metabolomics, or small‑molecule panels—were acquired on instruments using Electrospray Ionization (ESI) or MALDI coupled to quadrupole, Time‑of‑Flight (TOF), or Orbitrap analyzers. Understanding the broad behavior of these combinations will help you interpret intensities, missing values, and identification confidence in the later chapters.

1.2 What the instrument actually measures

1.2.1 Ions, not neutral molecules

Despite common language about “weighing molecules,” a mass spectrometer never measures the mass of neutral species. It manipulates ions—molecules that have been given one or more charges—because only charged particles respond to electric and magnetic fields. Every experiment begins with an ionization step that converts molecules in a solid, liquid, or gas sample into gas‑phase ions stable enough to traverse the instrument.

Once ionized, these species can be accelerated, focused, and filtered inside regions held at pressures as low as (10^{-5} – 10^{-8}) torr. This high vacuum minimizes collisions with background gas, which would otherwise scatter or neutralize the ions before they reach the detector. Conceptually, you can think of the instrument as a pinhole camera for ions: only those that follow particular trajectories under controlled fields are recorded.

1.2.2 The meaning of m/z

The fundamental output of the instrument is the mass spectrum, a plot of signal intensity versus (m/z). Here, (m/z) is defined as the mass of an ion (in Daltons) divided by its integer charge number (z). It is a dimensionless quantity and is the only quantity directly measured by the analyzer.

For singly charged ions ((z = 1)), the x‑axis can be read directly as mass plus or minus the mass of the acquired charge. However, many soft ionization techniques, especially ESI, generate multiply charged ions. A 40 kDa protein that acquires 20 protons will appear at (m/z ), not at 40,000. In such cases, the same molecule appears as a series of peaks corresponding to different charge states, and software must deconvolute these to neutral masses.

Understanding that the instrument measures (m/z), not mass per se, explains why:

  • The same analyte can produce multiple peaks at different (m/z).
  • High‑resolution instruments can distinguish ions with very similar masses but different compositions.
  • Adduct formation (e.g. ([M+Na]^+)) and in‑source fragments complicate naive interpretations of spectra.

Later chapters will make extensive use of this distinction when interpreting isotope patterns, charge states, and adducts in real datasets.

1.2.3 Spectra and chromatograms

In hyphenated techniques like LC–MS and GC–MS, the instrument acquires spectra continuously during a chromatographic separation. This creates two complementary views of the data:

  • A Total Ion Chromatogram (TIC), plotting the sum of all ion intensities versus retention time. Peaks in the TIC correspond to groups of compounds eluting from the column.
  • Individual mass spectra at specific time points, plotting intensity versus (m/z) for the ions present at that moment.

Data analysis tools exploit both dimensions. Peak detection algorithms search for coherent signals across time at specific (m/z) values, producing extracted ion chromatograms and integrated peak areas. Database search engines in proteomics match observed MS/MS spectra to theoretical fragment patterns. In later chapters, we will show how these low‑level representations are transformed into feature matrices and identification tables suitable for statistical modelling.

1.3 From sample to digital data

1.3.1 The instrument as a five‑step pipeline

Although commercial instruments differ in implementation, almost all can be described as a five‑step pipeline:

  1. Sample inlet introduces a small portion of the sample into the instrument without collapsing the vacuum system.
  2. Ion source converts neutral molecules into gas‑phase ions.
  3. Mass analyzer separates ions according to (m/z).
  4. Detector converts ion impacts into electrical signals.
  5. Vacuum system maintains low pressure so ions can travel without excessive collisions.

For our purposes, the critical pieces are the ion source, mass analyzer, and data acquisition mode, because they leave recognizable signatures in the data you will analyze.

1.3.2 GC–MS, LC–MS, and MALDI as archetypes

Three archetypal configurations generate most of the datasets encountered in hands‑on analysis:

  • GC–EI–MS: Gas chromatography with Electron Ionization. Volatile, thermally stable small molecules are separated in the gas phase, then ionized by a high‑energy electron beam that produces rich fragmentation spectra. This is common in environmental analysis and metabolite profiling of volatile compounds.
  • LC–ESI–MS(/MS): Liquid chromatography with Electrospray Ionization. Polar and semi‑polar small molecules, peptides, and proteins are separated in solution and ionized at atmospheric pressure from charged droplets. This is the workhorse for proteomics and metabolomics.
  • MALDI–TOF(/TOF): Matrix‑assisted laser desorption with Time‑of‑Flight detection. Solid‑phase analytes co‑crystallized with a matrix are desorbed by a pulsed laser and analyzed as singly charged ions, often in imaging or intact mass experiments.

Later, when we discuss specific R workflows (e.g. xcms for LC–MS, MSnbase/QFeatures for proteomics), we will revisit these archetypes to explain how their physics shapes peak shapes, charge states, and identification strategies.

1.4 Common ionization techniques (short orientation)

For data analysis, you do not need to memorize all ion sources, but it is helpful to recognize the major ones because they influence:

  • Which molecules are detectable at all.
  • Whether spectra are dominated by molecular ions or fragments.
  • Typical charge states and mass ranges.

1.4.1 Electron Ionization (EI)

Electron Ionization is the standard for GC–MS of volatile and semi‑volatile small molecules. Neutral analyte molecules in the gas phase are bombarded by 70 eV electrons, producing radical cations that often undergo extensive fragmentation. The resulting spectra are highly reproducible and form the basis of large spectral libraries used for compound identification.

Consequences for data analysis:

  • Spectra are rich in fragments; the molecular ion may be weak or absent.
  • Identification often relies on library matching rather than exact mass.
  • Data files typically contain full‑scan MS1 spectra with EI fragment patterns across the GC elution time axis.

1.4.2 Electrospray Ionization (ESI)

Electrospray Ionization is the dominant source for LC–MS of polar analytes, especially peptides and proteins. The LC effluent is sprayed through a high‑voltage capillary, forming charged droplets that evaporate and emit gas‑phase ions. ESI is a soft technique, usually preserving the intact molecular ion while generating characteristic multiple charge states.

Consequences for data analysis:

  • Peptides and proteins appear as charge state envelopes; deconvolution or charge inference is required.
  • Spectra often show adducts (e.g. ([M+Na]^+)), which must be handled in preprocessing.
  • Most modern proteomics and metabolomics formats (DDA, DIA) are LC–ESI–MS/MS based, and the packages you will use are tuned to this landscape.

1.4.3 MALDI

MALDI ionizes analytes from a solid matrix using a pulsed laser, producing predominantly singly charged ions even for high‑mass species. When coupled to TOF analyzers, it offers extremely fast, high‑throughput acquisition over wide mass ranges.

Consequences for data analysis:

  • Spectra are simple, with one peak per species rather than charge envelopes.
  • Intensities can be influenced by local matrix heterogeneity and crystallization, which affects quantitative interpretation.
  • In imaging experiments, each pixel has its own spectrum, creating very large datasets with spatial structure.

APCI and other sources play important roles but can usually be treated as variations on ESI from a data perspective: LC–MS with predominantly singly charged small‑molecule ions and relatively simple isotopic patterns.

1.5 Common analyzers and what they imply

Mass analyzers determine resolution, mass accuracy, and the way scans are acquired over time. For analysts, the key question is whether the data are low‑ or high‑resolution and whether they come from a scanning filter or a pulsed, all‑masses‑at‑once device.

1.5.1 Quadrupole

Quadrupole analyzers use electric fields between four rods to transmit only ions of a narrow (m/z) window at a time. By sweeping voltages, they scan across a mass range or hold a fixed (m/z) for targeted monitoring. Resolution and mass accuracy are modest but adequate for many targeted assays.

Data‑level consequences:

  • Full‑scan quadrupole data contain one spectrum per scan, built up by sequentially stepping through (m/z).
  • In targeted modes (SIM, MRM), only a limited set of (m/z) traces is recorded, yielding highly sparse but precise data.

1.5.2 Time‑of‑Flight (TOF)

TOF analyzers accelerate ions to a common kinetic energy and measure their flight times down a field‑free tube. Lighter ions arrive sooner than heavier ones, and the measured times are converted to (m/z). With a reflectron to correct for energy spread, TOF instruments achieve high resolution and good mass accuracy at very high acquisition speeds.

Data‑level consequences:

  • Spectra cover wide (m/z) ranges with many data points per unit time, which affects storage size and processing time.
  • Q–TOF hybrids combine quadrupole selection with TOF detection, enabling high‑resolution MS/MS helpful for precise identification in omics workflows.

1.5.3 Orbitrap

Orbitrap analyzers trap ions in electrostatic fields and infer (m/z) from the frequency of ion oscillations, which is independent of initial ion energy. Fourier transformation of the induced image current yields high‑resolution mass spectra with sub‑ppm accuracy over wide mass ranges.

Data‑level consequences:

  • MS1 and MS/MS spectra can be extremely information‑rich, with finely resolved isotopic patterns and low mass errors.
  • Many identification and quantification algorithms exploit this HRAM property—for example, by requiring narrow mass tolerances or using exact‑mass constraints in metabolite annotation.

In this book, when we speak of “high‑resolution” data, we typically refer to Orbitrap or Q–TOF‑based datasets; when we speak of “triple quadrupole data,” we have targeted MRM workflows in mind.

1.6 Acquisition strategies and their impact on data

Perhaps the most important experimental choice for a data analyst is the acquisition strategy, because it dictates what information is present and how it is distributed across the file. We distinguish between untargeted modes, which aim to characterize many species at once, and targeted modes, which optimize for precise quantification of predefined analytes.

1.6.1 Untargeted acquisition: Full Scan, DDA, DIA

Full‑scan MS

In full‑scan mode, the instrument repeatedly acquires MS1 spectra across a defined (m/z) range throughout the chromatographic run. This is the simplest untargeted strategy and provides a complete view of ionizable species at each time point, but offers no direct structural information beyond isotopic patterns and adducts. It underpins many small‑molecule profiling workflows where identification is based on accurate mass and retention time.

Data‑Dependent Acquisition (DDA)

DDA adds structural detail by interleaving MS1 scans with MS/MS scans triggered in real time. After each MS1 scan, the instrument software selects the top N most intense precursors for fragmentation; these are isolated, fragmented, and analyzed to provide product ion spectra. Dynamic exclusion prevents the same precursors from being repeatedly selected over a elution peak.

From a data perspective:

  • Files contain interlaced MS1 and MS/MS scans.
  • Identification is based on matching MS/MS spectra to peptide or small‑molecule libraries.
  • Quantification often relies on MS1‑level extracted ion chromatograms for identified features.

DDA is widely used in discovery proteomics but exhibits stochastic sampling of lower‑abundance species, which contributes to missing values across replicate runs.

Data‑Independent Acquisition (DIA)

DIA seeks to capture fragmentation information for all detectable species in a reproducible way. Instead of isolating individual precursors, the instrument steps through a series of wide (m/z) windows (e.g. 20–25 Da each), fragmenting all ions within each window and recording composite MS/MS spectra.

From a data perspective:

  • Every run contains systematic fragment information across the whole mass range, improving reproducibility and coverage.
  • Product ion spectra are multiplexed: fragments from many co‑isolated precursors appear together, requiring specialized deconvolution algorithms and often prior spectral libraries.
  • DIA yields dense data matrices well‑suited to statistical analysis but requires more sophisticated preprocessing pipelines.

Throughout the second half of this book, we will show how data structures differ between DDA and DIA and how R packages handle each type.

1.6.2 Targeted acquisition: SIM, SRM/MRM

Targeted methods restrict acquisition to predefined analytes, trading global coverage for sensitivity and quantitative precision.

  • Selected Ion Monitoring (SIM) narrows MS1 acquisition to specific (m/z) values, increasing dwell time and improving signal‑to‑noise for those ions.
  • Selected/Multiple Reaction Monitoring (SRM/MRM) on triple quadrupoles goes further: Q1 selects a precursor (m/z), q2 fragments it, and Q3 monitors a characteristic product (m/z). Only ions that pass both filters contribute to signal, greatly reducing matrix interference.

From a data standpoint, MRM files contain chromatographic traces for a small set of predefined transitions (precursor–fragment pairs), each of which is typically integrated and modelled separately. These data often show few missing values, broad dynamic range, and high precision—ideal conditions for regression and mixed‑effects modelling that we will exploit in the targeted‑omics chapters.

1.7 Sample preparation: the “garbage in, garbage out” principle

For a book on data analysis, sample preparation may seem peripheral, but it directly shapes missingness patterns, dynamic ranges, and artefacts. Three points matter most:

  1. Ion suppression and contamination are real. Non‑volatile salts, detergents, and other contaminants compete with analytes for charge in ESI sources, suppress signals, and foul the instrument. This leads to lower sensitivity, changed relative intensities, and more missing values.
  2. Clean‑up improves quantitative behavior. Methods such as solid‑phase extraction selectively retain analytes while removing matrix components, reducing ion suppression and improving reproducibility.
  3. Proteomics uses standardized bottom‑up workflows. Denaturation, reduction/alkylation, tryptic digestion, and peptide desalting produce samples with predictable peptide populations and mass ranges. Understanding that your data originate from such workflows helps explain the peptide‑level structure of identification tables and the presence of shared peptides across proteins.

In this book we assume that sample preparation and acquisition have been carried out competently. Nonetheless, many real‑world artefacts in downstream data can be traced back to upstream issues; wherever possible, we will highlight those connections in the applied chapters.

1.8 From raw files to feature matrices (bridge to the rest of the book)

Mass spectrometers from different vendors produce proprietary formats, but most can be converted into open standards such as mzML, mzXML, or MGF. These formats encode:

  • Instrument metadata (ion source, analyzer, acquisition mode).
  • Spectra (lists of (m/z) values and intensities for each scan).
  • Chromatographic information (scan times, retention time indices).

Within the R ecosystem, packages such as Spectra, MSnbase, xcms, psmtools/PSMatch, and QFeatures provide an increasingly high‑level interface: from raw spectra to detected features, from peptide–spectrum matches to protein‑level quantitative tables. Subsequent chapters will walk through:

  • Importing mzML/mzXML and exploring raw spectra.
  • Performing chromatographic peak picking and alignment.
  • Building feature matrices and performing quality control.
  • Carrying out statistical tests and visualization tailored to proteomics and metabolomics.

The concepts introduced here—(m/z), ionization, analyzers, acquisition modes, and sample quality—form the physical backdrop against which all those computational operations take place. With this orientation in mind, we now turn to the practical side: how to work with real MS data in R.
